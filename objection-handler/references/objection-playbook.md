# Objection Playbook

## The ACCA Framework (Apply to Every Objection)

```
A — Acknowledge  → Validate without agreeing it's a dealbreaker
C — Clarify      → Ask a question to surface the real issue
C — Connect      → Tie your response to their stated pain or goal
A — Advance      → Move toward the next step
```

---

## Objection 1: Price / Budget

### "It's too expensive."

**Acknowledge:**
"I hear you — budget is always a real consideration."

**Clarify:**
"When you say expensive, is it that the price is higher than you expected, or that you're not yet sure the ROI justifies it?"

**Connect (if ROI unclear):**
"Let's put it in context. [Customer X] was spending [Y hours/dollars] on [problem]. Within [timeframe] they saw [result]. If similar numbers apply to you, the math usually works out. Want to run through it together?"

**Connect (if budget genuinely unavailable):**
"Understood. Is this a 'not right now' budget situation, or 'never' — meaning it's not a priority regardless of price?"

**Advance:**
"If we could show a clear ROI model tied to your numbers, would that change the conversation?"

---

### "We don't have budget for this."

**Clarify:**
"Is the budget truly not there, or is it allocated elsewhere? I ask because sometimes teams find budget when the pain is high enough."

**Reframe:**
"What's the cost of NOT solving this over the next 12 months? Sometimes that math changes the budget conversation internally."

**Advance:**
"Would it help if I put together a business case you could bring to your leadership?"

---

### "We can get something cheaper."

**Acknowledge:**
"There are definitely cheaper options out there — you're right."

**Clarify:**
"What's important to you beyond price? What would a cheaper tool need to do for it to be a real option?"

**Connect:**
"A lot of teams that started with [cheaper alternative] came to us after [specific failure mode]. The total cost including [rework/switching/lost time] often ends up higher."

**Advance:**
"Would it make sense to do a side-by-side on total cost of ownership before you decide?"

---

## Objection 2: Timing

### "Not now — come back next quarter."

**Acknowledge:**
"Timing matters and I don't want to force something that isn't ready."

**Clarify:**
"Help me understand — is it that the priority isn't there right now, or that you're waiting for something specific to change?"

**Connect:**
"The teams I talk to who push to next quarter sometimes find the same conversation waiting for them. What would need to be true for this to be a priority now?"

**Advance:**
"Can we at least put a placeholder in the calendar so we don't lose the context we've built?"

---

### "We're too busy right now."

**Clarify:**
"I get it — what's driving the busyness? Is it a specific project or initiative?"

**Reframe:**
"Often the problem we solve is what's creating the busyness. Is [the pain we address] part of what's overwhelming the team?"

**Advance:**
"What if we started with a minimal setup that takes [X hours] of your time? You'd have something running while you focus on the current priorities."

---

## Objection 3: Competitor

### "We already use [Competitor X]."

**Acknowledge:**
"[Competitor X] is a solid tool — a lot of teams use it."

**Clarify:**
"What do you love about it? And is there anything it doesn't do well that you've worked around?"

**Connect:**
"What we hear from teams that use [Competitor X] is [specific gap]. If that's not an issue for you, then [Competitor X] is probably fine. But if [gap] is something you're dealing with, that's exactly what we built for."

**Advance:**
"Would it be worth a 20-min call just to see if there's a gap worth addressing?"

---

### "We're evaluating [Competitor X] too."

**Acknowledge:**
"That makes sense — you should absolutely compare."

**Clarify:**
"What are the key criteria you're using to evaluate? What matters most?"

**Connect:**
"We tend to win when [specific criterion] is the priority. Where does that rank for you?"

**Advance:**
"Would it help if I sent you a comparison guide on exactly those criteria?"

---

## Objection 4: Authority

### "I need to check with my boss."

**Clarify:**
"Of course. What's your read on how they'll see it?"

**Connect:**
"Is there anything you'd want me to help you prepare for that conversation? Sometimes a quick business case or one-pager makes it easier."

**Advance:**
"Would it make sense to get them on a call together so we can answer any questions directly? That often moves things faster."

---

### "This isn't my decision."

**Clarify:**
"Who does own this decision? And what's your role in the process — do you have influence or input?"

**Connect:**
"I want to make sure we're including the right people. Who should I be talking to?"

**Advance:**
"Can you make an introduction, or would it be easier if I reached out directly?"

---

## Objection 5: Need

### "We don't really have that problem."

**Clarify:**
"Tell me more — what does your current process look like for [area]? I want to make sure I'm not assuming."

**Reframe:**
"Sometimes the problem exists but hasn't been quantified yet. What would [the pain] actually cost if it happened?"

**Advance:**
"Fair enough. Is there a problem we haven't talked about that IS keeping you up at night? I'd rather focus on what's real."

---

### "We're fine with what we have."

**Clarify:**
"What does 'fine' mean — is it working well, or is it working well enough that the pain isn't worth addressing yet?"

**Reframe:**
"A lot of the teams we work with said the same thing 6 months before switching. The tipping point was usually [specific trigger]. Have you hit anything like that?"

**Advance:**
"Would you be open to a no-pressure benchmark? Just to see if there's a gap worth knowing about."

---

## Objection 6: Trust / Credibility

### "We've never heard of you."

**Acknowledge:**
"We're not the biggest name in the space — yet."

**Connect:**
"[Recognizable customer] said the same thing before trying us. Here's what they found: [result]."

**Advance:**
"Would it help to talk to one of our customers directly? I can set up a reference call."

---

### "You're too small / not enterprise-ready."

**Clarify:**
"What specifically concerns you — support, security, reliability, contract terms?"

**Connect:**
"We're SOC 2 certified / have 99.9% uptime SLA / have enterprise contracts in place. Which of those is the key one for you?"

**Advance:**
"Let me put you in touch with [enterprise customer similar to them] — they had the same concern."

---

## Objection 7: Complexity / Risk

### "This seems hard to implement."

**Clarify:**
"What part worries you most — the technical integration, internal change management, or timeline?"

**Connect:**
"Most teams are live within [X days]. Here's what onboarding looks like: [3 steps, concrete timeline]. [Customer] was up and running in [timeframe] with [small team size]."

**Advance:**
"Want me to set up a call with our implementation team so you can hear directly what's involved?"

---

### "We don't have bandwidth for this."

**Reframe:**
"I hear you. The irony is that [our product] is usually what gives bandwidth back — [Customer] freed up [X hours/week] within [timeframe]."

**Clarify:**
"What would make this feel low enough effort to be worth it? Is it a lighter onboarding, a phased rollout, or something else?"

---

## Handling Stacked Objections

When a prospect throws multiple objections at once:

1. Don't address them all — you'll sound defensive
2. Acknowledge: "Those are all fair points."
3. Prioritize: "Of everything you mentioned, which one is the biggest concern?"
4. Solve that one fully before moving on

---

## When to Walk Away

Not every deal is worth chasing. Re-qualify if:
- Same objection persists after 3 genuine attempts to address it
- No clear champion or economic buyer will engage
- Timeline keeps moving with no commitment
- Pain isn't strong enough to justify the change

Ask directly: "I want to be honest with you — is this something you see moving forward, or should we revisit in [timeframe]?"
